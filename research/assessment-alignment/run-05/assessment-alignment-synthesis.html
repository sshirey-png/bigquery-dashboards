Assessment Alignment Synthesis

FirstLine Schools Network · 2024-25 School Year · n = 1,890 LEAP-tested students

Executive Summary

  • anet interims are the most reliable early-warning system FirstLine has. BOY scores predict LEAP outcomes with r = 0.63-0.69, cleanly separating all five achievement levels. An anet BOY score below 30% catches 81-83% of students who will score Unsatisfactory on LEAP (n = 1,840 ELA, 1,787 Math).
  • Course grades are dangerously misleading in Math. 451 students (24.2%) carried a B+ or higher in Math but scored Unsatisfactory or Approaching Basic on LEAP. Grade 6 Math is the epicenter: 44% of 6th graders have inflated Math grades network-wide (n = 325).
  • SPED students receive the most inflated signals. SPED students average a 79.9% Math course grade but only 6.5% reach proficiency on LEAP — a 73 percentage point gap between what grades say and what students can actually do (n = 170, 3 schools).
  • MAP is statistically stronger but operationally limited for Math. MAP Math percentile correlates at r = 0.84 with LEAP Math — the highest of any single predictor — but only 26% of LEAP-tested students have MAP Math BOY scores. A composite (30% anet + 70% MAP) gains only +0.017 over MAP alone. Expand MAP Math administration or rely on anet.
  • Network proficiency peaked in 23-24 and fell back. ELA: 27.1% → 31.0% → 28.4%. Math: 16.9% → 22.1% → 19.7%. The 23-24 gains were not sustained. LHA declined in both subjects over the two-year window. Wheatley showed the most consistent improvement.
  • Grade 5 Math is a structural collapse point. Math proficiency drops by 10-30 percentage points between grades 4 and 5 at every school, reaching 2.4% at Wheatley (2 of 83 students proficient). The content transition to fractions and multi-step operations is defeating students network-wide.
  • Science proficiency is flat at ~12% across three years. No internal assessment system is aligned to or predicts Science LEAP outcomes. This is a measurement and instructional blind spot.

Key Finding

The composite predictor offers marginal gains over the best single instrument. Combining standardized anet BOY and MAP BOY percentile scores (optimal weight: 35% anet / 65% MAP for ELA, 30% / 70% for Math) yields r = 0.759 for ELA (n = 1,808) and r = 0.850 for Math (n = 500). These improve over the best single predictor by +0.029 (ELA) and +0.017 (Math). The gain does not justify the added complexity of composite scoring for operational use.

5. School-Specific Recommendations

Ashe Arthur Ashe Charter School

LEAP tested: 534 | ELA prof: 29.8% | Math prof: 18.0%

1. Math grading is completely disconnected from standards. r = 0.147 between Math grades and LEAP. Grade 7 Math grades anti-predict performance (r = -0.17, n=99). This is not a calibration issue — it is a measurement failure. Grades must be rebuilt around standards-referenced criteria, using anet scores as an external anchor.
2. 29% of Math students receive inflated grades — the highest rate of any school. 153 students carry B+ or better but scored U/AB on LEAP. Priority: share anet data with Math teachers and families for these students immediately.
3. MAP ELA is the strongest predictor at this school (r = 0.757). Continue MAP ELA administration. Consider expanding MAP Math coverage to fill the data gap.

Green Samuel J. Green Charter School

LEAP tested: 338 | ELA prof: 30.2% | Math prof: 24.0%

1. Green has the best grade alignment of any school (Math r = 0.695). Whatever grading practices Green uses should be studied and shared network-wide. Green's approach is the existence proof that standards-aligned grading is achievable at FirstLine.
2. SPED Math inflation is catastrophic: 51.4% (18 of 35 students). This is the highest SPED inflation rate in the network. Green's regular-ed grading may be aligned, but SPED grading is not. Audit SPED Math grading practices specifically.
3. Highest Math proficiency of any school (24.0%). Green's Math instructional model is outperforming the network. Investigate what Green does differently in Math — curriculum, pacing, teacher quality, or assessment practice.

LHA Langston Hughes Academy

LEAP tested: 519 | ELA prof: 25.0% | Math prof: 20.0%

1. LHA is the only school that declined in both ELA and Math over 2 years. ELA: 25.9% → 31.2% → 25.0%. Math: 21.7% → 27.3% → 20.0%. The 23-24 gains were an anomaly. Root cause analysis needed: was the 23-24 spike driven by a single cohort, a staffing change, or a testing artifact?
2. ELA grade inflation is the worst in the network (21.5%). 110 of 511 students have inflated ELA grades. LHA is the only school where ELA inflation exceeds 15%. Focus on ELA grading calibration specifically.
3. LHA has the most On Track Math students (43%), driven by strong 3rd grade anet performance (67.8% BOY Math). Protect and build on this strength — these students are the pipeline for future proficiency gains.

Wheatley Phillis Wheatley Community School

LEAP tested: 499 | ELA prof: 26.1% | Math prof: 18.4%

1. 48% of ELA students and 47% of Math students are in Crisis or Intervention tiers. This is the highest concentration of at-risk students in the network. Wheatley needs the most intensive resource allocation for intervention support.
2. Grade 5 Math: 2.4% proficiency (2 of 83 students). This is a catastrophe that demands immediate investigation. Is it a staffing issue, curricular gap, or student readiness problem? The content transition to fractions and multi-step operations is defeating nearly every student.
3. Wheatley showed the most consistent 2-year improvement: ELA +3.6pp, Math +6.1pp. Despite having the lowest baseline, the trajectory is positive. This school is doing something right in overall growth. Identify and protect those practices while addressing the Grade 5 Math crisis.

6. Limitations and Open Questions

What This Study Cannot Answer

  • Causation. Correlations between internal assessments and LEAP do not prove that anet or MAP measure the same construct as LEAP. They may share variance from a common latent factor (e.g., general academic ability) rather than measuring specific standards. Intervention decisions based on this alignment should be evaluated with outcome data.
  • MAP Math at scale. Only 26% of LEAP-tested students have MAP Math BOY scores. All MAP Math findings (including the r = 0.84 correlation) are based on a self-selected subsample. The true network-wide relationship may differ.
  • LHA demographics. LEAP _full tables (which contain SPED, EL, and economic status) do not exist for LHA (school 399005). All demographic concordance findings cover only 3 of 4 schools (n = 1,385 of 1,890). LHA-specific equity gaps are unmeasured.
  • Teacher-level grading patterns. We identified school-level grade inflation but cannot determine whether it is systemic (shared norms) or concentrated in specific teachers. The teacher_assignment_summary table in grades could enable this analysis but was not pursued in this study.
  • Temporal stability. All concordance findings are from a single year (2024-25). We do not know if these relationships are stable across years. The 23-24 LEAP data exists and could be used for cross-validation in a future study.
  • Science and Social Studies prediction. No internal assessment system was found to predict Science or Social Studies LEAP outcomes. Science proficiency is flat at ~12% across three years. There is no internal early-warning system for these subjects.
  • Student mobility. 85 students (4.5%) appeared in multiple LEAP school tables, indicating mid-year transfers between FirstLine schools. These were deduplicated, but the transfer experience itself may affect performance in ways this analysis does not capture.
  • Performance Matters as a predictor. PM's 875 unique test names across different schools and teachers make cross-school comparison impossible. PM is useful for formative classroom-level decisions but cannot serve as a network-wide predictor.
  • K-2 early warning. This study covers grades 3-8 (LEAP-tested grades). K-2 students take DIBELS, not LEAP. Whether anet/MAP K-2 scores predict future LEAP performance is untested.

Data Needed for Future Studies

  • LHA LEAP _full table — to enable demographic equity analysis for all 4 schools
  • Expanded MAP Math administration — from 26% to 90%+ coverage to validate MAP Math as a network-wide predictor
  • Teacher-level grade distributions — to identify which teachers contribute most to inflation
  • 23-24 internal assessment data linked to 23-24 LEAP — to cross-validate concordance stability across years
  • 25-26 BOY/MOY data linked to eventual 25-26 LEAP — to test the risk tiers prospectively rather than retrospectively
  • DIBELS data linked to future LEAP outcomes — to extend the early-warning system to K-2
  • Science-specific internal assessments — PM has Science content, but coverage and standardization are insufficient for prediction

7. Data Quality & Methodology Notes

Item Detail
Population 1,890 unique LEAP-tested students across 4 FirstLine schools, 2024-25
Deduplication 85 students appeared in multiple school LEAP tables; kept highest-scoring record
Join key LASID (State Student Number), STRING type in all tables used
anet aggregation Item-level data aggregated: SUM(points_received)/SUM(points_possible) per student per assessment
Grades bridge grades.Student_Number → roster.Student_Number → roster.State_StudentNumber (LASID). 98.7-99.1% match rate.
School name normalization LIKE-based CASE matching; 4 canonical names (Ashe, Green, LHA, Wheatley) derived from up to 3 variants per school
Grade threshold Y1 (full-year) grades used; course names matched by pattern (ELA/English, Math/Algebra I)
LEAP data cleaning 3 records with blank ELAAchievement excluded; SchoolName trailing whitespace trimmed
Correlation method Pearson product-moment correlation between continuous scores; no imputation for missing data
Concordance method Binary proficient/not-proficient classification at each threshold; Cohen's kappa for chance-corrected agreement
Composite predictor Z-scored anet BOY + MAP BOY percentile; weights optimized by grid search (0.05 increments) on 2024-25 data
